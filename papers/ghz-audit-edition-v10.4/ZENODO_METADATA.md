### 2. Abstract (Audit Declaration)
This research presents a rigorous post-selection audit of the Greenberger-Horne-Zeilinger (GHZ) violation. Historically cited as the definitive evidence for quantum non-locality (F=4.0), we demonstrate that these results are highly sensitive to detection thresholds and amplitude-gating rules. By introducing a matched-retention random control, we prove that the apparent "violation" is a statistical artifact of data filtering (post-selection amplification). We propose that "entanglement" may be a geometric mathematical illusion, with profound implications for the physical foundations of quantum computing.

### 3. Core Findings
- **Selection Sensitivity**: We demonstrate that F=4.0 is not an intrinsic mechanism value but is coupled to the "Selection Tax" (discarded samples).
- **Random Control Benchmark**: Using the `V10.4` real cost curve, we show that amplitude-gated gains far exceed random subsampling baselines, identifying the specific "accounting rules" that generate apparent non-locality.
- **Geometric Artifact**: The GHZ violation is reinterpreted as the geometric closure of local wave fields, suggesting that quantum computing may be solving geometric problems rather than utilizing physical entanglement.
