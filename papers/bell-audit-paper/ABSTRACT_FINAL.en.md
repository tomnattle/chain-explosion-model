# Bell External Abstract (English)

We present a denominator-audit framework for Bell/CHSH statistics to separate mechanism effects from protocol-bookkeeping effects under a shared data context. The study evaluates strict and standard protocols in parallel and explicitly separates decision criteria into an engineering gate (computability and data sufficiency) and a thesis gate (preregistered claim constraints), preventing direct promotion of runnable outputs into publication-level claims. Under the NIST-index configuration, we obtain `S_strict=2.336276` and `S_standard=2.839387`: the engineering gate passes, while the thesis gate fails, primarily due to strict-bound violation. A simulated fallback configuration further shows that `S_standard` does not necessarily exceed `S_strict`, reinforcing strong protocol sensitivity to windowing and sample-inclusion rules. We therefore report a method-level conclusion: a single high `S` peak is not claim-complete evidence for ontology-level extrapolation without jointly satisfying protocol definitions, gate criteria, and negative-result consistency. The framework is intended to improve auditability and reproducibility for high-claim statistical interpretations in Bell/CHSH analyses.
