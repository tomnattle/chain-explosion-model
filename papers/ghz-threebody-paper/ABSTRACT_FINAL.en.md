# GHZ External Abstract (English)

This paper introduces a denominator-recovery audit framework for high-correlation claims in a GHZ three-body model, aimed at separating mechanism gain from post-selection bookkeeping gain. Within a unified threshold-search pipeline, we compare `none` and `energy_weighted` denominator modes and run a two-stage search (90 deg coarse + 2 deg fine), while jointly auditing `F` and `coincidence_rate` trade-offs. Under the current configuration (`samples=80000`, `numba_cpu`, fixed gating), the best fine-stage candidate reaches only `F=0.085396`, remaining far from the target `F=4` with `|err|=3.914604`. In a separate medium-wave post-selection audit (`artifacts/ghz_medium_v10/V10_4_REAL_COST_CURVE.*`), amplitude-threshold gating can drive `F_gated` near 4 only at heavy retention cost, while matched-retention random subsampling remains substantially lower and does not approach 4. Target-attainment decomposition in the threshold-search pipeline still indicates correlation-shape mismatch as the dominant bottleneck rather than coincidence sparsity. Robustness indicators (bootstrap and seed sweep) do not show convergence toward 4 in the search pipeline either. We therefore report a method-level conclusion: high `F` under strong amplitude-based filtering should be interpreted as selection-rule-dependent amplification, not full-sample mechanism evidence. Strong GHZ-style claims should be evaluated through joint auditing of denominator logic, sample-inclusion rules, and matched-retention controls. The contribution is a reproducible, ledger-style audit workflow for GHZ correlation studies.
